Documented Cases of Orchestrated Loyalty Tests

Your suspicion isn’t coming out of nowhere – there are real instances of partners orchestrating “loyalty tests” using decoys or friends. In fact, some individuals (often driven by insecurity or mistrust) have covertly arranged for a third party to flirt with or approach their partner to see how they’d respond. For example, one man discovered his girlfriend had her attractive friend send him flirty DMs as a “loyalty test”, something she claimed is “a common thing” women do to “prove [their partner’s] loyalty”. He was stunned, thinking this kind of setup “only [happened] in movies, not in real life”. In another case, a woman shared that her boyfriend asked a friend to hit on her online to test her fidelity, then declared she “failed his loyalty test” because she responded politely instead of blocking the decoy immediately. These stories illustrate that coordinated decoy dates or flirtations do occur in real life, usually in an attempt to catch a partner being unfaithful or to “manufacture betrayal” as proof of disloyalty.

Crucially, relationship experts warn that setting up such loyalty traps is a sign of deep trust issues and manipulation. It’s broadly viewed as toxic behavior. As one commentator put it, “If someone has to manufacture betrayal to feel secure, they’re not looking for love, they’re looking for control”. Psychology professionals echo that “testing” a partner is destructive – it undermines genuine communication and trust. Partners who feel the need to secretly test devotion are often insecure and trying to “gain proof” of love, but these tests are essentially dishonest manipulations that tend to backfire.

In short, orchestrated loyalty tests do happen (your scenario has parallels in other people’s experiences), but they are widely considered unhealthy and indicative of underlying relationship problems.

Given this context, it’s plausible that Adriana and her circle could have coordinated something similar. The red flags you noted align with known loyalty-test setups:

  • a sudden drop in her communication after a commitment talk (possibly to create a scenario to “tempt” you)
  • an out-of-the-blue approach by her acquaintance (Maria) soon after
  • later discovering Maria was connected to Adriana’s social circle

This doesn’t prove it was a scheme, but it matches patterns seen in anecdotes of loyalty tests.

However, it’s important to approach this objectively. We should weigh two main interpretations of Adriana’s behavior and the Maria incident: one rooted in attachment style and relationship anxiety, and another rooted in deliberate emotional manipulation. We’ll also examine whether gaslighting or covert control tactics appear to be at play.

Attachment Dynamics: Avoidant Behavior vs. Insecure “Testing”

One interpretation is that Adriana’s behavior might be explained by attachment style issues (possibly avoidant or anxious-avoidant) rather than a calculated plot. Let’s break down her actions in that light:

  • After the serious commitment talk, she pulled back communication. This is very typical of an avoidant attachment pattern – when intimacy increases or a relationship deepens, an avoidantly attached person can feel overwhelmed or fearful of being engulfed, leading them to withdraw or create distance. A dismissive-avoidant person often needs space after emotional closeness, which could explain her reduced texting/calling without requiring any secret scheme. In attachment terms, the commitment discussion may have triggered a “fear of intimacy”, causing her to retreat emotionally.
  • Her later hot-and-cold behavior maps onto a classic push-pull dynamic. In a push-pull cycle, one partner oscillates between “pulling” close with affection and vulnerability, and then “pushing” away with distance or aloofness. This results in exactly the emotional whiplash you describe – periods of warmth and attachment bids, followed by withdrawal and silence. Such behavior can stem from conflicting desires: craving intimacy and reassurance one moment, then feeling fear of dependency or loss of autonomy the next.
  • The timing of Maria’s entry could be coincidental (or gossip-driven) rather than orchestrated. For instance, if Adriana told some friends about your commitment talk and her uncertainty, it’s possible Maria (a mutual acquaintance) learned you might be newly “free” or on a break and decided to ask you out on her own initiative. This would still be inappropriate friend behavior, but not necessarily masterminded by Adriana.
  • It’s also possible Adriana has mixed avoidant + anxious tendencies. Sometimes called fearful-avoidant, this can show up as a craving for reassurance but also a fear of closeness. Such individuals may engage in “tests” or odd behaviors driven by insecurity, even if they aren’t consciously setting a trap. This can become an unintentional form of manipulation born of anxiety, rather than a cold, calculated move.

In summary, Adriana’s withdrawal and mixed signals could be explained by avoidant or anxious attachment behaviors. The push-pull cycle you experienced – closeness then distance – is a well-documented pattern that can arise from fear of intimacy, insecurity, or a desire for control (even subconscious desire). If this attachment-based interpretation is correct, her actions weren’t necessarily coordinated with others to trap you, but rather were self-protective moves or protest behaviors aimed at reassurance.

Her circle’s involvement with Maria might have been incidental or driven by gossip rather than a direct conspiracy. This interpretation leans towards “likely” if Adriana has a known history of anxiety or avoidance in relationships – the behavioral clues fit that mold closely.

Signs of Deliberate Manipulation: Loyalty Test, Triangulation, and Control

The alternative interpretation is that your gut is right – Adriana (and/or her friends) orchestrated an emotional setup as a form of covert manipulation or loyalty test. Several aspects of the situation align with tactics known in manipulative relationship dynamics:

The “Decoy” Date (Maria)

The way Maria entered the picture is highly suspect. It wasn’t just a random new person; she turned out to be directly connected to Adriana’s social network, and her timing was almost too perfect (appearing right after Adriana pulled away). This strongly suggests coordination.

In known loyalty-test scenarios, it’s common to employ a friend or acquaintance as a decoy precisely because they can approach naturally and later report back. Your scenario – where Maria actively asks you on a date out of the blue – fits this pattern of setting a honey trap.

Triangulation

If this was orchestrated, Adriana was effectively engaging in triangulation, a known manipulation strategy. Triangulation means one person brings a third party into the relationship dynamic to manipulate or control the situation. In romantic contexts, a classic triangulation move is to introduce another “potential partner” (or use a friend) to stir up jealousy, test the partner’s reaction, or assert power.

By having Maria take you on a date, Adriana might have been trying to observe whether you’d remain loyal or be easily swayed – essentially “tipping the scales” in her favor by manufacturing a scenario. It can become a no-win setup:

  • If you refuse Maria’s advances, Adriana gets reassurance that you’re devoted.
  • If you accept, Adriana gets justification for pulling away or punishing you emotionally.

In both outcomes, the instigator maintains a form of control and the targeted partner gets confusion and emotional instability.

Intermittent Reinforcement & Control

You mentioned that after the date incident, Adriana became less responsive but still occasionally pulled you back in with intense emotional moments or invitations. This pattern can be a form of intermittent reinforcement, a manipulation tactic where positive feedback/affection is given unpredictably amidst negative or neutral behavior. Psychologically, this unpredictability can deepen attachment because it keeps you “chasing” the next good moment.

Whether she did this consciously or not, the effect is often the same: emotional rollercoaster, heightened longing, and greater dependence.

Gaslighting (Possible Signs)

Gaslighting involves distorting someone’s reality and making them doubt their own perceptions. Do we see signs of gaslighting here? Potentially, yes, in the covert nature of the situation. If Adriana arranged the loyalty test but never acknowledged it – instead acting like her distance or emotive swings have nothing to do with Maria – she’s leaving you to question your own narrative.

True gaslighting would become clearer if there were a direct confrontation and denial of facts/feelings (for example: “You’re being paranoid,” or “Nothing changed,” despite clear behavioral shifts). We don’t know if that confrontation happened. But secrecy + punishment without truth can function like covert control, because it keeps you confused and reactive.

Covert Control Motives

Consider why someone would do all this. Often it boils down to insecurity, fear, or power. A person with abandonment fears might test their partner to gain (false) certainty. Others may enjoy the “one-up” feeling of controlling the narrative.

One key missing element (so far) is an explicit reveal. In many loyalty test stories, the tester eventually admits it (“I had my friend hit on you”). Here, Adriana hasn’t openly cited the Maria date as a reason for her behavior. That could mean:

  • It wasn’t a test (and her coldness has other causes), or
  • It was a test, but she’s keeping it hidden to avoid accountability.

Either way, the dynamic can still be damaging: inconsistent reinforcement, uncertainty, and a lack of transparent communication.

Objective Breakdown: How Likely is an Organized “Loyalty Test”?

Considering all the above, let’s assess the plausibility with a balanced view of likely, possible, and unlikely explanations:

Likely (Straightforward Attachment/Personality Explanation)

  • The pattern of Adriana’s behavior (excluding the Maria twist) is highly consistent with an avoidant or fearful-avoidant attachment style.
  • Distancing after a commitment talk can be a normal avoidant reflex to intimacy, not necessarily a scheme.
  • The push-pull of intense closeness and withdrawal is a known relationship dynamic that can reflect internal conflict or immaturity rather than calculated cruelty.
  • Even if a “test” happened, the likely driver is insecurity (attachment insecurity / low self-esteem / fear of abandonment), not pure malice.

Possible (Coordinated Loyalty Test Theory)

  • The coincidences are hard to ignore: Maria’s sudden interest timed with Adriana’s withdrawal, plus the discovered social link.
  • Similar known cases have the same elements: decoy, secrecy, and a shift in behavior after the decoy interaction.
  • The post-incident ambivalence could fit a situation where the “results” weren’t black-and-white, creating ongoing uncertainty.
  • Without admission or proof, it remains circumstantial — but still plausible given the alignment of timing + connections.

Unlikely (Other Explanations or Exaggerated Interpretations)

  • It’s unlikely that a whole group orchestrated this purely to cause pain “for sport” with no grounding in insecurity or genuine feelings.
  • It’s also unlikely that Maria’s appearance was pure coincidence with zero information transfer, given the overlap in social circles.
  • It’s unlikely that Maria had no impact on Adriana at all, even if the date wasn’t orchestrated.

The least likely scenario is that nothing about Maria affected Adriana — human social networks tend to transmit information quickly, especially when people share followers/friends.

Conclusion: Attachment Issues vs. Manipulation – Finding Clarity

In evaluating your experience, it seems plausible that a coordinated loyalty test occurred, yet it also overlaps with known attachment-driven behaviors. It may not be an either/or so much as a both/and: Adriana likely has real commitment/attachment issues (avoidance, insecurity) which led to her pulling away, and those same issues could have driven her to sanction a loyalty test through her friend.

The hallmarks of an avoidant or fearful partner are evident (the push-pull cycle, mixed signals, inability to communicate needs directly), which suggests her emotional volatility is genuine. However, the hallmarks of covert manipulation are also present (deception via a third party, observing your reaction from the shadows, then indirectly using it against you).

So how to reconcile this? Often insecure individuals resort to covert maneuvers because they lack healthy communication tools. Adriana might not be a cartoon villain; she might be a confused young woman who thought “setting up a loyalty trap” would give her clarity or comfort. Unfortunately, such tactics usually backfire.

For your own clarity and closure, focus on the core issues:

  • Trust — is it mutual and assumed, or constantly tested?
  • Communication — are needs and boundaries stated openly, or implied through silence?
  • Respect — are you treated as a person, or as a “case” to be evaluated?

If you ever choose to address it directly, what matters most is her reaction to calm, factual observations and your need for clarity. Denial, blame-shifting, or “you’re imagining it” responses would be significant red flags. Honest ownership and a willingness to fix patterns would be the only path toward rebuilding trust.

Bottom line: the organized test theory is plausible — real cases exist and your story has meaningful parallels. At the same time, many parts of the behavior pattern are also consistent with attachment dynamics. Even without certainty, the situation shows dysfunctional relationship patterns that can harm you over time.
